The “tone-deaf” numbers

What this piece is: a sceptical look at how we count "tone-deafness" — and at the way even the kind debunking ("only ~1.5% are truly amusic, so almost anyone can sing!") might still do harm. Not aimed at the student; a room to think honestly, including against your own side's favourite stat.

The core worry that makes this matter (the real argument)

The debunk backfires. "Only 1.5% are truly tone-deaf" still concedes that there IS a born-broken category — it just makes it small. And a frightened person doesn't hear "1.5%"; they hear "so there's a hard wall, and I might be on the wrong side of it." The category itself — not its size — is the thing that lets someone opt out before trying. That's the inversion worth writing.

The numbers to interrogate (claims, NOT facts to repeat)

~1.5% amusia — Peretz & Vuvan 2017 (large sample, ~20,000). The headline debunk stat.

~0.52% — Pradilla et al. 2020 (Colombia, 383 students). Even lower.

~17% SELF-report tone-deafness — Cuddy et al. 2005 (one university sample, ~2,000).

The old "4%" — a single test from 1980 (Kalmus & Fry). The figure everyone quotes; weak.

⚠ Verify EVERY one of these against the primary source before publishing — you're writing a piece that accuses these numbers of being "thrown around," so your own handling has to be airtight. Questions to push: what does the test actually measure (pitch discrimination ≠ "can't learn to sing")? Is amusia trainable / does it improve with practice? Does a perception deficit even predict a production deficit? Who got excluded from each sample?

Open questions (yours to sharpen)
