Your fuller answer (verbatim, 9 Jul 2026 — the positive account)

I think that to me the word "singing" is not value-neutral. Much like "natural", it can be weaponised - an "expert" or "authority" can use this word to classify certain vocal expressions as singing or not singing. Perhaps a child playing with their voice and inventing rhymes can be seen and recognised as singing, but if an adult does the same thing, that's just considered them being insane. Or rhytmicised speech (which obviously has its own patterning in terms of higher and lower sounds) is rejected as "not music" or "not song" in order to elevate the Western canon. This is often invisible and automatic - the person who says "let's sing now" might not be themself aware that they are reinforcing a value judgment. What they might actually mean is "I would like something with more familiar structure that is more widely recognised as singing in my cultural circles" - obviously a mouthful. However, to negate the playfulness of one's own vocal expression (inventing rhymes, playing with the sound of words or even non-verbal sounds produced by the vocal apparatus) is to impose self-judgment (what I do doesn't qualify as singing). So in my opinion, what qualifies as singing is situational - to "sing like an animal" is considered a slur - unless one creates a specific context where this is permitted - and when one does, all of a sudden there is room for innate creativity, for play, for a release of energy.

Claude labels: "singing is not value-neutral ... like 'natural', it can be weaponised" ties straight to the singing-methods essay's critique of "the natural voice." The child-vs-adult double standard + "rhythmicised speech rejected as 'not music' to elevate the Western canon" = the gatekeeping thesis again. The positive turn: singing is SITUATIONAL, and a permissive context unlocks "innate creativity, play, a release of energy" — that's the same no-judgment mechanism as lessons-raw §10 (the circle-song).
