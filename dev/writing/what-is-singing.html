"What does it mean to sing?" Your fuller answer (verbatim, 9 Jul 2026 — the positive account)

I think that to me the word "singing" is not value-neutral. Much like "natural", it can be weaponised - an "expert" or "authority" can use this word to classify certain vocal expressions as singing or not singing. Perhaps a child playing with their voice and inventing rhymes can be seen and recognised as singing, but if an adult does the same thing, that's just considered them being insane. Or rhytmicised speech (which obviously has its own patterning in terms of higher and lower sounds) is rejected as "not music" or "not song" in order to elevate the Western canon. This is often invisible and automatic - the person who says "let's sing now" might not be themself aware that they are reinforcing a value judgment. What they might actually mean is "I would like something with more familiar structure that is more widely recognised as singing in my cultural circles" - obviously a mouthful. However, to negate the playfulness of one's own vocal expression (inventing rhymes, playing with the sound of words or even non-verbal sounds produced by the vocal apparatus) is to impose self-judgment (what I do doesn't qualify as singing). So in my opinion, what qualifies as singing is situational - to "sing like an animal" is considered a slur - unless one creates a specific context where this is permitted - and when one does, all of a sudden there is room for innate creativity, for play, for a release of energy.

Interview round 2 — your answers (verbatim, 17 Jul 2026)

1. On the choir moment / what the sirens "lacked" (answer to Q1)

I think that we are so immersed in equal-temperament music that we don't recognise anything outside of it as "song" - or maybe not as "real song" - someone might hum a melody with little accuracy, but if someone sings that same melody in equal temperament right after, this will get recognised as "the actual song" immediately - because it points at a memory of something that was originally in equal temperament. But maybe there is a particular expectation on the adults - either you sing the way everyone else sings (and the way everything is tuned), or that's not singing. In Polish there is a term "radosna tworczosc" ("joyful creativity"), which is often used in a derogatory sense - something which is spontaneous but outside of the acceptable norm, perhaps "cringe" is the closest anglophone idea I can think of. So maybe the question becomes - "what counts as song and when". Obviously nobody expects whales and children to sing in equal temperament - but an adult is measured against a societal standard

2. The permissive context — democratic circle-singing (answer to Q2)

One permissive context is the "democratic cicle-singing". A group of people who trust each other is divided into three sub-groups, and a person from the group gives a loop for each of the three to sing. When they're done - crucially - they invite another person to the circle and take their place (based on who hasn't been to the centre in a long time). The group is instructed to stay in time as whole - what this does is it gives an opportunity to "even out" the loop, to tune the intervals, to find a resonant consensus, to "define what we mean melodically and rhythmically". It reifies the "repeated speech as song" idea [that paper reference here].

The reference Matt wants = Diana Deutsch's speech-to-song illusion (Deutsch, Henthorn & Lapidis 2011, JASA; first noted Deutsch 2003) — already pinned in the seeds above. Claude label: this answer coins tuning-as-consensus — "in tune" as agreement among the people present, not conformity to an external grid.

3. The animal / what "produced music" edits out (answer to Q3)

There is an age-old human tendency to distance themselves from anything animalistic - to construct systems and rules of behaviour that signify to humans that they are human - be it with a dress code, architectural arrangements, or tonal systems. I feel like people without musical training would associate "human song" with something familiar - the concept of "in tune" is a tacit cultural agreement. There will be many cultures where the focus won't be on pitch, but more on the rhythmic context and the curve of the line - things that would get edited-out in "produced music"

Interview round 3 — responses to the four theses (verbatim, 17 Jul 2026)

A. The cover thesis — SIGNED, and extended

Yes, absolutely - the "I don't sound like the recording". "I must sound like the recording" - "I must replicate what is already out there as accurately as possible", which also means that I judge anything that doesn't fit my idea of "safe", in myself and also in others, by proxy. A hum is its own special moment - but is often neglected as "trying to be like recording". Ever so often I've heard people say things like "X has a really good voice - they should go on a talent show", as if the point of singing was to be judged and to compete. This to me speaks of losing sight of the participative amount of song, and makes me feel sad.

Claude label: "the participative amount of song" → direct bridge to the Turino participatory/presentational spine (participatory-music-turino-notes.md). The judging-others-"by proxy" move is new: the internalised standard polices outward too.

B. The consensus thesis — REWRITTEN: the body has its own tuning (just intonation)

There is a kind of tuning that people are capable away from their instruments - just intonation, which pretty much all a cappella styles leverage. The intervals are tuned according to their frequency ratios, and you don't need a piano to get people singing intervals of octaves or perfect fifths - they will often drift to them because they sit comfortably in their range, and because they sound close to a unison - inexperienced singers with an unexplored vocal range will often sing in parallel fifths to a melody, from my experience, without realising it

Claude label: this REVISES tuning-as-consensus — tuning isn't only social agreement, there's a bodily attractor (frequency ratios, drift to octave/fifth, near-unison comfort). Killer observation: the inexperienced singer's "wrong" parallel fifths are just-intonation tuning done unknowingly. NB 12-TET is itself a compromise away from those ratios — the ruler the adult is graded by is detuned relative to the body's defaults.

C. The licence thesis — generalised to normativity

I think this touches on a more general question of normativity, of associating beauty with sameness, of fear of originality or individuality. Doing something jarring is a risk, and public shaming is something everyone tries very hard to avoid.

D. The species thesis — CAUTION FLAG on the Tonebank bridge

I'd be very careful to associate Tonebank with animality, because of issues of colonialism and epistemic violence. I would say though that giving musicians the tools with natively operate outside of the worn 12-TET, 4/4 chord changes and straight/shuffle swing has the potential to broaden the scope of expressivity and get people to listen in a different way.

Claude label: important correction — do NOT link African musical systems to the "animal" pole (colonial trope). Matt's usable framing: tools to operate NATIVELY outside worn 12-TET / 4-4 / straight-shuffle = broadened expressivity, "get people to listen in a different way".
