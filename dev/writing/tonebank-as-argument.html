Most music plugins made by tech people are quite cerebral — they focus on abstractions rather than on particularities. The electronic-music lineage is informed by a community which does not have much experience with the gestural dimension of music: the focus is on texture, on the spectrum, on form — its dance is culturally specific to an aesthetic linked to technology. As a musician I understand this as an aesthetic. But with music, dance and performance being something I have experience with — especially in the participative, collective dimension: circle dances, choral singing, percussion bands — I feel that the seated body produces music that is very different than the dancing body. We take our embodied experiences to music-making.

Tonebank — the Afro-Harmonic Sequencer I was the founding developer of — is, among other things, an argument about exactly this. The MVP was about generating loops and phrases based on specific African musical traditions — crucially, modelling systems where the harmonic change doesn’t happen on bars, half-bars, etc., but where it’s related to the “bell pattern”, which we express as groups of 2 and 3 subdivisions. This results in naturally-syncopated structures — often there is no sound on the beat, which is what makes the body move. Our paradigm was to look closely at the physicality of the expression that we’re familiar with, and to give tools which open that world to the electronic musician — making the acoustic, old, embodied traditions something valuable, treasured and foundational.

With the translation, I think it’s about making models of embodiment. Duple- and triple-time subdivisions — skipping and stepping rhythms — are certainly to do with our bipedalism. The swing has to do with the shape of our arms, their degrees of freedom, the mechanics of hitting a drum or playing a piano key — the expressivity dimension of making music. A 3223 swing has longer divisions where the movement of the shaker in samba is with the arm, shorter where it’s with the wrist. But this is obviously a very crude model — the whole body is involved, including the lower body, which for cultural reasons is often invisibilised in the global north.

With swing it’s the same thing everywhere you look closely. In Poland, where I’m from, folk music in one village can differ in subdivision lengths from another because of people’s very-local dialects. There is a huge multiplicity of possibilities beyond mechanistic divisions. Musicians know this; tech people don’t — but often they think they do: their mental models will not be nearly as accurate as the practitioners’, because of a lack of embodied experience.

And underneath the models sits something no model reaches. A totally honest answer, which I have heard many music practitioners agree with: the “why” cannot be explained in words, quantified. It is the joy of rhythmic tension and resolution interplaying with the internal senses of your own body — going back to Shakira’s “hips don’t lie” idea — and the inseparability of music and dance and theatre.

So I think the hardest part is the social aspect: it’s for the tech people — who are used to working with systems which are clear, efficient and “objective” — to open to the wisdom and diversity of how music has been made in the non-digital world; not with an extractive mentality, but with a willingness to learn. The decision to honour the roots of the project is reflected in the tagline “Afro-Harmonic Sequencer”. We took great care to name our sources and give credit in the manual — specific traditions, where our knowledge comes from, the fact that we didn’t grow up in those traditions but that we have a passion for it. Personally, this is the most valuable thing to me: situating ourselves, and being open about where the knowledge we are passing comes from. What we are doing is approximating our own embodied experience — creating a commercial product which reflects it with care and a level of detail.

In that sense, the coders should be the “beginners”: there is always room for musical growth for coders, and for technical growth for musicians. Maybe the biggest barrier is the commonly-shared preconceptions about one’s own capacities — music and math being seen as something like “innate talent” rather than a learnable ability.

Giving musicians tools which natively operate outside of the worn 12-TET, 4/4 chord changes and straight/shuffle swing has the potential to broaden the scope of expressivity — and to get people to listen in a different way.
