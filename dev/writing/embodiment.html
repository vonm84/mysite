Embodiment — the moving, situated singing body

What this piece is: the home for the body argument that the methods essay only nods to. The thesis seed: most Western vocal training assumes a still, upright, waist-up singer — and immobilising the body is an intervention, not a neutral default. Singing and movement are, in much of the world, one thing. This is the essay closest to YOUR lived practice (Rhythms of the City, samba, Boal, dance, martial arts).

The seeds from the research (the frame — keep the citations, lose the jargon)

"Pinning a butterfly." Fixing the singing body in stillness is an intervention on something whose nature is to move — and which dies, in a sense, once pinned. EMPIRICAL FLOOR: posture and stability measurably change breathing/phonation (Traser 2021 — chest expands ~20% more supine; Calvache 2022 — unstable surface shifts pitch). So immobilising a singer has real consequences; it is not a zero-point.

The pelvis/hips taboo. A historically Western (and colonial) body-politics split the body into a "respectable, expressive" upper zone (hands, face) and a "shameful, sexual" lower zone (pelvis, hips) — then disciplined the latter into stillness. (Gottschild documents this in Black performance.) ⚠ EMPIRICAL vs INTERPRETATION: that movement changes phonation = fact; that immobilisation carries colonial meaning = argued interpretation. The essay must keep these two clearly apart.

Two bodies, contrasted. The erect European spine as a single upright "unit" (all movement generated from the centre) vs the Africanist body that is polycentric (many centres of movement at once, including the pelvis) and polyrhythmic (different parts to different rhythms). — Brenda Dixon Gottschild; Kariamu Welsh.

Body-orality / the anti-fragmentation epistemology (the Brazilian spine). Toledo — corpo-oralidade, the Black body NOT to be cut into parts to be understood. Martins — oralitura (knowledge carried through the moving, voicing body) and tempo espiralar (spiral, not linear, time). Sodré — samba's syncopation (the "missing" strong beat the body completes) as bodily knowledge and resistance. → song and hip-movement are ONE practice: samba de roda, candomblé, forró, konnakol, bellydance, circle/round dances. To "sing" without moving is the strange, artificial act.

The Western somatic family ≠ the universal cure. Alexander, Feldenkrais, Body Mapping (Conable), Lessac, Dalcroze, Laban take the body seriously and put it in motion — but they are Western frameworks too. Don't crown them the neutral fix; that repeats the very error the essay is noticing. One family among several.
