Singing lessons for people who have been told they couldn't sing

You are not alone.

I've had many, many students (in the hundreds), and the story you are telling me is the story me and other singing teachers have heard thousands of times. As one of my teachers, the fantastic Naomi Susan Isaacs (her TEDx talk here), has noticed — music seems to be almost the only subject where we are graded before we are taught.

Did anyone ever give you an opportunity to just try singing, guide you, give you things to try, follow your development without judgment and preconceived ideas? The fact that you're coming to me means that there is something inside you that KNOWS that singing IS something you have the right to, and that what was done to you was unjust.

The truth is, we are all very unique, and a thing that works for one person doesn't work for another. Some people grew up in homes full of music and singing, others didn't have a habit of singing — and so many mistake it for natural "talent", especially with the advent of TV talent shows and various "singing methods" being sold as the only panacea.

You have a voice, and it's unique and original, and it's wanting to get out. I promise to you I will do my absolute utmost to create the conditions under which it can grow, make you feel safe, share my experience and skills to support your learning, and be absolutely honest with you. I will NOT give up on you, because I believe that your voice is a gift, and I am curious to hear it and see you shine.

My take on teaching singing

I do not believe in "good/bad" when it comes to the voice.

We start with a conversation. The "why" to me is more important than the "how" — and the conversation itself is already a meaningful exchange; we can already hear each other's voices. I believe that you can shape the voice to sing in a certain style, acquire classical technique, study belting or jazz improv or whatever, or sing your own songs — but none of this matters at this stage. What matters is what brings you here. What matters is me hearing from you what it is that lights you up. Giving you the space to share your stories of singing, of your voice being experienced by others — maybe family or friends, maybe a wider community of people around you who want to hear you, maybe finding the points that light you up, that give you passion, and then getting you to articulate any beliefs that someone might have imparted on you that you've held up until now.

My role is to give space and to listen. To give room for your voice to reverberate — and I don't mean that just in a traditional, technical sense. There are plenty of singers who don't have "conventionally big" voices, whose message and whose voice resonate deeply because they are expressing something personal, some truth. I want to know your favourite songs, your dreams about singing, what it is like for you to experience your own voice.

Only from there will we develop a path that is unique to you, and uniquely useful to you. You might be more interested in pop music, or in musicals, or in a particular artist — anything that motivates you will be the vehicle for our lessons. You will be allowed to do what gives you pleasure, and you will be encouraged to discover that pleasure in singing.

I will be very honest about the emotional impact that your journey has on me, and basically be a kind and encouraging audience. I might suggest ways of singing, or particular exercises, that I think might help you — but this will not be an expectation. I will be with you on a journey of discovering what works for you, and I have solid experience and many, many ideas to try, and it will bring me immense joy to share these to empower you and help you let yourself be heard.

How it works

The first step is just a chat — friendly, free, no strings. First contact is always very approachable: a conversation where we see if this is the right fit. I want to hear about your interests, your dreams, what music you listen to, what you'd like help with. It's very personal.

I don't promise a miracle cure — but I can offer ideas to try, specific exercises that will help: working on tuning, rhythm, voice emission, accompanying yourself on an instrument, songwriting, or whatever you would like.

There will be things to do in between lessons — learning the lyrics of songs, trying specific online or recorded exercises. It's about incremental progress: not like cramming a book, more like learning a language — by immersion, with a lot of practice, learning to treat “mistakes” as an indication of which direction to go next, rather than taking them personally.

An ideal start is a 30-minute lesson once a week. There are discounts if you book five or ten lessons in advance.

Once a month or so there's an informal get-together with small presentations — even if that means each person just sings a song they like to everyone, unaccompanied. Very low-pressure and community-oriented — and it's included in the price of your lessons.
